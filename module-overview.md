## Module DATDRD05-T05 – Decision Intelligence and AI Agents

This study unit is part of the Minor Data Driven Decision Making in Business at HAN University of Applied Sciences. The module spans **7 weeks** and is structured around a single applied professional task that runs throughout.

The course focuses on how AI Agents and Large Language Models (LLMs) can support business decision-making in modern organisations — not on building pipelines for programmers or AI Engineers, but on enabling students to **design, evaluate, and responsibly apply AI-supported decision agents**, with explicit attention to privacy, legal constraints (e.g., GDPR), ethics, bias, and organisational governance.

A central metaphor used throughout is the **"intern analogy"**: the AI agent is like a smart, autonomous intern — active, not passive, working through an agentic workflow by breaking tasks into steps, letting AI handle some steps, with humans retaining full accountability for all decisions.

## Learning Outcomes

Assessment criteria (aligned with CRISP-DM phases) define what students are expected to demonstrate. After completing this module, students can:

**1. Business Understanding — Decision Context & Business Case**
- Clearly define a decision problem in a business context
- Identify stakeholders, decision-makers, and organisational impact
- Formulate decision objectives and success criteria
- Explain how an AI-supported agent improves decision quality, efficiency, or scalability
- Build a convincing business case and justify why AI is appropriate for the decision

**2. Data Understanding — Data Requirements for the Decision Agent**
- Assess data availability, quality, and limitations
- Explore how data supports decision variables, signals, or predictions
- Reflect on data ownership and governance

**3. Data Preparation — Structuring Data for Decision Support**
- Explain how data should be cleaned, structured, or transformed
- Define features, indicators, or inputs needed by the agent
- Address data management issues such as consistency and reliability

**4. Modelling — Design of the AI-Supported Decision Agent**
- Design the architecture of the decision agent in a flow chart
- Explain the AI techniques or algorithms used (or proposed)
- Show how the model supports the decision-making process
- Design a human-in-the-loop system
- Justify model choice and complexity
- *(Optional for higher grades:)* Build a working prototype and demonstrate the agent performing a simplified decision task

**5. Evaluation — Performance, Risks, and Ethical Reflection**
- Evaluate how well the agent supports decision-making
- Define evaluation metrics (accuracy, usefulness, business impact, etc.)
- Identify limitations and uncertainties
- Critically reflect on ethical, privacy, and data-security risks: bias, fairness, privacy risks, data security, responsible AI use, transparency and explainability
- Propose risk mitigation strategies

**6. Deployment — Implementation & Organisational Integration**
- Describe how the decision agent would be implemented in an organisation
- Explain workflow integration and user interaction
- Identify organisational requirements (training, monitoring, maintenance)
- Reflect on scalability and future improvements

## Weekly Programme & Learning Activities

The course follows a 7-week schedule with class sessions that combine short lectures, discussion, and in-class work:

**Week 1 – Decision Framing & Agent Purpose**
Topics: Decision statement, Decision context, Agent role definition, Human-in-the-loop design. Students learn what an AI-supported decision agent is, what it is *not* (it's not an autonomous action-taking bot like OpenClaw), and how to define their business case. A first homework assignment is introduced.

**Week 2 – Agent Architecture ("Keep It Simple")**
Topics: Introduction to different types of AI use in agents (RAG, Image-to-text, Speech-to-text, Classification), examples from marketing and customer service (e.g., FAQ-based Social Media Responder, E-Mail Triage Agent). In-class assignment: students use a provided template to define their agent's role (part 1) and draw its architecture as a flow chart (part 2).

**Week 3 – Guest Speakers from Practice**
Jorn van Schagen and Hakim Shaibu present the agentic solutions they built for real companies, sharing tools, resources, and lessons learned. Recommended resources introduced: w3schools.com, deeplearning.ai, learn.microsoft.com, and Lisa Crosbie on YouTube (Microsoft 365 Copilot agents).

**Week 4 – Elaboration, Feedback & Assessment Preparation**
Further development of business case and agent design. Feedback sessions (teacher and peer feedback), review of assessment criteria and rubric. Students are guided on how to elaborate their report following CRISP-DM structure and start writing. Assessment criteria are discussed in depth. This week also introduces the deadline (report: Monday 30 March 9:00h AM) and presentation date (Monday 6 April).

**Week 5 – Handling Data Responsibly & Cybersecurity**
Subject lesson covering: data privacy and security risks, bias in data and analytics, ethical considerations (transparency, fairness), data governance risks, compliance risks, technical risks, human error, explainability, impact assessment. Also covers cybersecurity best practices. Includes a Tableau BI tool tutorial (20 minutes) and instructions for a weekly practical assignment.

**Week 6 – BI Deployment Vision & Project Management**
Subject lesson covering: evaluating a vision for BI deployment in an organisation, Managing Data and Analytics Projects, Project Management principles, Scrum and Agile methodology. Includes a Tableau BI tool tutorial and weekly practical assignment.

**Week 7 – Presentation Power BI**
Students present their work / Power BI dashboard. This week focuses on delivering and presenting findings.

## Assessment

Students prove their acquired knowledge and capabilities through a **single integrated professional task**, assessed via a **criterion-referenced interview (CBI)** supported by three deliverables:

1. **Written Report** — structured according to the 6 CRISP-DM phases (Business Understanding, Data Understanding, Data Preparation, Modelling, Evaluation, Deployment). Guideline: 6 paragraphs, approximately 0.5–1 page per paragraph. Deadline: Monday 30 March, 9:00h AM. Upload via HANdin.

2. **Architecture / Prototype Agent** — a flow chart showing the agent's architecture. A working prototype is optional (required for the highest grades only, score 7–10).

3. **Presentation + CBI** — a 20-minute pitch/demo session (Monday 6 April, students choose their own timeslot). Assessors: Meike Botterweg and Oliver Ntenje. The CBI covers the agent's business case and design, the CRISP-DM approach, and critical reflection on ethical and data-security risks. The presentation is assessed on two components: a *Decision Intelligence grade* and a *CRISP-DM grade (20%)*. Students may work individually or in pairs.

**Grading scale:**
- **0–2:** Insufficient performance — unclear problem, unrealistic business case, no coherent agent design, ethical issues not recognised
- **3–4:** Limited understanding — problem identified but poorly justified, unclear architecture
- **5.5–6:** Satisfactory/Good conceptual design — clear business case, relevant data identified, clear architecture, human-in-the-loop considered, ethical risks recognised
- **7–8:** Strong design with a working prototype — same as above plus a working, simplified prototype
- **9–10:** Excellent work with an advanced agent prototype — deep understanding of business value, sophisticated human-in-the-loop design, critical ethical analysis, complex and well-functioning prototype beyond a proof-of-concept

**Important note highlighted throughout the course:** "A simple, well-designed agent with clear governance will score higher than a complex model nobody understands." Attendance of at least 80% of classes is mandatory.
