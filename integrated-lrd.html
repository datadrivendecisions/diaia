Part 1 — Context & positioning

1.1 What this LRD is, and isn't

This document does not re-argue the mission (the EU competitiveness case, Draghi, the Competitiveness Compass) or re-list the seven SME-facing themes — both live in ai-for-smes-programme.html and stand as written. It does not re-derive the three technical sketches — they live in module-redesign-sketches.md. What this LRD adds:

  • A formal Learner Requirement Document for the two modules together, in the format HAN already uses for workshop and application design (see the LRD-Workshop precedents in background-docs/) — learning outcomes, design criteria, functional/non-functional requirements, deliverables, acceptance criteria.
  • A committed technical level for AEAIS: Sketch 3 — "Agentic Engineering & AI Systems." DIAIA correspondingly takes Sketch 3's 4b variant: pure strategy / economics / society, no build.
  • The integrated learning-path system (Part 6) — the new contribution this LRD makes on top of both source documents: a KISS mechanism that uses an AI Socratic tutor and a mandatory cross-module peer partnership to auto-regress each team's path through the module, without any bespoke adaptive-difficulty engine.

Part 2 — Pedagogical quality system

Four grounded frameworks (three in background-docs/education-design/; the fourth sourced from the AI Wiki) discipline every design choice in this LRD. They are stated here as design axioms, not summarised as literature.

2.1 Bloom's revised taxonomy — precision in what we ask for

Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) cross a six-level Cognitive Process Dimension (Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create) with a four-level Knowledge Dimension (Factual → Conceptual → Procedural → Metacognitive). A learning outcome is a verb (process) plus an object (knowledge type) — never a learning activity in disguise.

Design axiom: every learning outcome in Part 3 is tagged with its Bloom cell. A module that only asks students to describe or explain (Understand) is not doing what a capstone-driven minor needs; both modules here climb to Evaluate and Create, and — because the gating system in Part 6 explicitly asks teams to reflect on their own revision process — into metacognitive knowledge, the dimension classic rubrics rarely reach.

2.2 Motivation & Flow — why gates are calibrated, not arbitrary

Pink's Motivation 3.0 (Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose) operationalises Deci & Ryan's Self-Determination Theory; Csikszentmihalyi's Flow adds the experiential mechanism — a task must sit in the narrow channel between boredom (too easy) and anxiety (too hard), which maps directly onto Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development. Feedback must be informational ("here's how to improve") not controlling ("here's your grade") to sustain intrinsic motivation.

Pink motivatorMechanism in this LRD
AutonomyTeams choose their theme/angle (DIAIA) and their harness/knowledge-architecture approach (Technical); the gate never dictates how to solve the checkpoint, only whether the thinking behind it is visible.
MasteryGates are binary and calibrated per week (Part 6.1) so a team is never blocked by a target it has no realistic path to — the checkpoint artifact scales with what that week's content actually taught, keeping teams inside the flow channel rather than the anxiety or boredom bands.
PurposeEvery checkpoint is real handbook/platform work, not a rehearsal exercise — the "why" is never abstract (§Motivation and Flow, structural manifestations: Challenge Based Learning's Engage→Investigate→Act arc is the same shape as a gated week).

2.3 UnBlooms — grade the thinking, not the output

Austin (2026) argues that generative AI collapsed the assumption baked into every Bloom pyramid — that you must climb through Remember/Understand/Apply before you can Create. A student can now open an AI tool and skip straight to a polished artifact. Her response is not to invert the pyramid but to replace it with a metacognitive reflection loop — Questioning → Generating → Critiquing → Refining, organised around human agency and curiosity as the thing being protected, with "AI on the sidelines" providing productive friction through Socratic tutoring. The assessment question is never what did you produce; it is how deliberately did you think along the way.

Design axiom: this is the direct ancestor of the Socratic AI agent in Part 6.2. It is why that agent is contractually barred from ever telling a student their work is good, bad, or scored — verdicts short-circuit the reflection loop UnBlooms exists to protect. The loop's four stages (Questioning / Generating / Critiquing / Refining) map onto this LRD's cycle: the agent questions, the team generates a revision, the paired sister team critiques, the team refines — and only then does the gate open.

2.4 Durable skills — the capability being protected

Globerson et al. (2026), synthesised on the AI Wiki's Durable Skills page, define a durable skill by three criteria: open-ended (not reducible to closed-form rules), socially/contextually situated (its performance depends on a specific group, organisation or domain), and slow-changing (it doesn't go obsolete every 18 months). The canonical cluster is Collaboration, Creativity and Critical Thinking — and Critical Thinking now explicitly includes AI-Supported Exploration and AI-Supported Verification as sub-skills: judging AI output is itself a durable skill, not a stopgap until the model gets better. McKinsey Global Institute's Skill Change Index (2025) corroborates from the labour-market side: judgment, negotiation and mentorship sit in the lowest-automation-exposure quartile, while narrow digital skills sit in the highest.

Design axiom: this is not a fourth independent framework bolted onto Bloom, Flow and UnBlooms — it is the answer to which capability those three are protecting. Every learning outcome in Part 3 is tagged with the durable skill it practises (§3.2–3.3), and the gate itself (Part 6.1) is reframed as weekly, structural practice of two specific sub-skills: AI-Supported Verification and Collaboration.

Part 3 — Learning outcomes (Bloom-tagged)

Verbs are drawn from the revised-taxonomy verb bank (§2.1). [Process / Knowledge] tags follow Anderson & Krathwohl's two dimensions. Every outcome is phrased as a user story — a student can <do>, so <value> — the same actor + goal + value shape used elsewhere in the LRD template family (see LRD_Ontwerpende_Onderzoeksvragen_v2.docx.md §4.1). The value clause is anchored to the student's future professional role (§3.1), not to the handbook artifact alone — the artifact is the practice ground, the role is the point. A third tag, Durable skill, names which §2.4 sub-skill the outcome practises — the capability that role will still need after the model underneath it changes.

Part 4 — Target audience & prior knowledge

There is no discipline-specific admittance requirement — the minor is open to any student with at least three years of prior university study. Two populations naturally apply: business-oriented students preparing for an AI- and data-driven environment, and IT/Engineering students seeking deeper Machine Learning knowledge who also value the business modules. Module choice is not a re-run of a student's degree — the programme encourages students to take the role that stretches them. A business-background student may choose AEAIS to build real ML/coding fluency, so they can collaborate with an engineer as a peer, not a translator; an engineering-background student may choose DIAIA to build genuine business fluency.

Team size. Both modules run in fixed teams for the module's duration (individually or in pairs per current DIAIA practice, extendable to small teams) — team stability is a precondition for the cross-module pairing in Part 6.3, which only works if both sides of a pair are stable across the 7 weeks.

Part 5 — Design criteria

5.1 KISS, stated as constraints

"Keep It Super Simple" is not an aspiration in this LRD — it is a set of things this system is not allowed to become, because each of the alternatives was considered and rejected during design:

  • No adaptive-difficulty algorithm. A tiered-branching or Socratic-depth-ladder system was considered and rejected in favour of a single binary gate per checkpoint — one mechanism, one rule, no scoring model to maintain or explain.
  • No student-visible scores. Students see open/locked and Socratic questions. Nothing numeric. This is a direct consequence of §2.3 (UnBlooms) as much as of KISS.
  • One Socratic-agent component, reused, not two. AEAIS builds it once (Part 6.2); DIAIA consumes it. No parallel, module-specific tutoring systems.
  • One pairing, stable for 7 weeks. Rotating peer pools were considered (for exposure diversity) and rejected — a stable 1:1 DIAIA↔Technical pairing is simpler to run, simpler to explain to students, and turns into a real working relationship rather than a transaction (Part 6.3).
  • Reuse existing infrastructure. The AI Wiki / Quartz template already specified in programme.html §3.2 is the platform. This LRD adds a gating layer and a pairing registry on top of it — it does not stand up new infrastructure.

5.2 What "manageable" means for a teacher

A single instructor dashboard (Part 6.4) must answer, for every pair, in under a minute: where are they, is the gate open, has an override been used, does the peer feedback look genuine. If a teacher cannot answer those four questions in under a minute per pair, the system has failed its own KISS test — regardless of how pedagogically elegant it is.

Part 6 — The integrated learning-path system

This is the new mechanism this LRD contributes. It has three parts that only work together: a gate (6.1), a Socratic AI tutor that is one half of every gate (6.2), and a cross-module peer pairing that is the other half (6.3).

6.1 Gated progression

Each of the 7 weeks has exactly one checkpoint artifact per team — for DIAIA, that week's chapter section; for AEAIS, that week's harness/architecture/eval component. A team cannot start the next week's checkpoint until both conditions are met on the current one:

Gate conditionWho/what checks itWhat "met" meansDurable skill (§2.4)
A — Socratic gateThe Socratic AI agent (6.2)The team has submitted a written response to the agent's questions that is a genuine revision, not a resubmission — the hidden rubric's process dimension (not polish) clears a fixed threshold.Critical Thinking — AI-Supported Verification
B — Peer gateThe paired sister team (6.3)The team has both given and received one round of feedback with its paired sister team on this week's checkpoint.Collaboration — Conflict Resolution, Project Management

Naming this matters: gate condition A drills the exact sub-skill — AI-Supported Verification — that the durable-skills literature (§2.4) flags as most at risk of atrophying without deliberate practice; gate condition B drills Collaboration's two most measurable sub-skills, every week, for seven weeks.

Both conditions, no partial credit, no numeric composite. This is deliberately the "gated progression" option, not tiered branching or a depth ladder (§5.1) — the autoregression is in whether a team advances, not in which content it sees next. Every team eventually sees the same seven checkpoints; what varies is how many Socratic/peer cycles it takes to earn the next one — which is exactly the "average ≥2 iterations" pattern already proven in the workshop-LRD precedent (see background-docs/LRD_Workshop_Onderzoekend_Vermogen.md §8, AC-03).

A locked gate is not a penalty. It carries no grade consequence by itself (Part 9) — it is Pink's informational feedback, not controlling feedback (§2.2): a prompt to revise, not a mark against the team.

6.2 The Socratic AI agent

Extends the agent already sketched in programme.html §5.3 — same dual-audience design, now also a gate condition rather than a pre-publication review step only.

  • To the team (visible): only Socratic questions. Never a verdict, never "this is good/bad," never a score. Questions target the UnBlooms loop directly — assumptions, evidence, what would still confuse an SME owner or break under load.
  • To the instructor (hidden): a rubric score against that week's CRISP-DM phase (DIAIA) or harness/ADLC layer (Technical), plus the binary gate-A signal. Students never see either.
  • Built by: AEAIS, as one of its own six learning outcomes (§3.3, #3 and #6) — the same team-pairing structure that gates DIAIA also applies here: the AEAIS team that builds this component is itself gated by Socratic + peer review on its own build.

6.3 Cross-module peer network

Every DIAIA team is paired, for the full 7 weeks, with exactly one Technical team. This is the "force optimal use of the peer network" mechanism: the peer network is not self-selected or optional — it is a structural, cross-disciplinary assignment, reviewed reciprocally every week.

DIAIA team reviews the Technical team's checkpoint for…Technical team reviews the DIAIA team's checkpoint for…
LensBusiness feasibility — would an SME owner trust and use this; does it fit the "first five steps" the chapter promises?Engineering soundness — does the "Recommended tooling" section in the chapter reflect what's actually buildable, at what effort?
PayoffDIAIA's handbook chapter gets a technically-grounded "Recommended tooling" section instead of a generic oneThe Technical team's build gets a real, business-articulated user story instead of a synthetic spec

Fallback. If one side of a pair falls behind or a team drops the module, the instructor reassigns the pairing rather than blocking the other team (FR-07, Part 7) — mirrors the persona/archetype fallback already used for SME access in programme.html §3.3.

6.4 Instructor dashboard

One screen, per pair: current week, gate-A / gate-B status, hidden rubric trend, iteration count, and an override control. The override exists because no binary gate should become a bureaucratic trap — an instructor can open a gate manually with one click, logged for the calibration record (Part 11, AC-07 bounds how often that should be needed).

Part 9 — Assessment alignment

Gates are formative; the criterion-based interview (CBI) stays the sole summative instrument, unchanged in shape from programme.html §5.4:

  • A locked gate never itself changes a grade. It is a revision prompt, consistent with Pink's informational-feedback principle (§2.2).
  • The Socratic agent's hidden rubric score is an instructor calibration signal only — exactly as already specified for the pre-publication review in programme.html. This LRD extends its use from "before publication" to "at every weekly gate," but does not change its evidentiary status.
  • At the CBI, a student defends not just the chapter/build but how they responded to the Socratic questions and to their paired sister team across the seven gates — directly assessing the metacognitive-knowledge outcomes in Part 3 (DIAIA #6, Technical #6).
  • The exported gate/iteration history (FR-08) is the evidence base for that defence — not a score sheet, a process trail.

Part 10 — Deliverables

  1. A published (curated) handbook chapter per DIAIA team, unchanged in shape from programme.html's chapter anatomy — now with a technically-grounded "Recommended tooling" section sourced from the paired Technical team.
  2. A working platform component per Technical team (harness + knowledge architecture + eval pipeline + deployment/monitoring), demoed at the capstone.
  3. The Socratic-AI-and-gating system itself, built once by (a) Technical team(s) as their own capstone-within-the-capstone.
  4. A per-pair exported gate/iteration/feedback history (FR-08), feeding both CBIs.
  5. An instructor dashboard (Part 6.4) usable from week 1 of the pilot onward.

Part 13 — Roadmap

  1. Pilot the gate on Theme 1 only. One cohort, week 1 checkpoint only, manual instructor grading of the Socratic transcripts before trusting the hidden rubric — confirms the mechanism before it runs unattended for 7 weeks.
  2. Stand up the pairing registry and dashboard (FR-05/06) alongside the week-1 pilot.
  3. Full 7-week gated run, one cohort. Both modules, all checkpoints gated, override logging live from day one.
  4. Calibrate the hidden-rubric threshold against AC-05/AC-07 at cohort end; adjust before the next intake.
  5. Compounding. Subsequent cohorts inherit the gate system as infrastructure — only the checkpoint content (chapter themes, harness scope) needs refreshing, per the living-artifact model already established for the handbook.

Suggested owners: course team (DIAIA + AEAIS), with AEAIS's capstone team owning the gate system's engineering as their own deliverable each cohort.
