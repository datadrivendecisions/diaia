Part 1 — Context & positioning

1.1 What this LRD is, and isn't

This document does not re-argue the mission (the EU competitiveness case, Draghi, the Competitiveness Compass) or re-list the seven SME-facing themes — both live in ai-for-smes-programme.html and stand as written. It does not re-derive the three technical sketches — they live in module-redesign-sketches.md. What this LRD adds:

  • A formal Learner Requirement Document for the two modules together, in the format HAN already uses for workshop and application design (see the LRD-Workshop precedents in background-docs/) — learning outcomes, design criteria, functional/non-functional requirements, deliverables, acceptance criteria.
  • A committed technical level for AEAIS: Sketch 3 — "Agentic Engineering & AI Systems." DIAIA correspondingly takes Sketch 3's 4b variant: pure strategy / economics / society, no build.
  • The integrated learning-path system (Part 6) — the new contribution this LRD makes on top of both source documents: every team owns one chapter end-to-end, feeding its chosen topic and data into its own agentic system against a shared, instructor-set quality bar; progression is gated by an AI Socratic tutor and a mandatory cross-team peer partnership, without any bespoke adaptive-difficulty engine.

Part 2 — Pedagogical quality system

Four grounded frameworks (three in background-docs/education-design/; the fourth sourced from the AI Wiki) discipline every design choice in this LRD. They are stated here as design axioms, not summarised as literature.

2.1 Bloom's revised taxonomy — precision in what we ask for

Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) cross a six-level Cognitive Process Dimension (Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create) with a four-level Knowledge Dimension (Factual → Conceptual → Procedural → Metacognitive). A learning outcome is a verb (process) plus an object (knowledge type) — never a learning activity in disguise.

Design axiom: every learning outcome in Part 3 is tagged with its Bloom cell. A module that only asks students to describe or explain (Understand) is not doing what a capstone-driven minor needs; both modules here climb to Evaluate and Create, and — because the gating system in Part 6 explicitly asks teams to reflect on their own revision process — into metacognitive knowledge, the dimension classic rubrics rarely reach.

2.2 Motivation & Flow — why gates are calibrated, not arbitrary

Pink's Motivation 3.0 (Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose) operationalises Deci & Ryan's Self-Determination Theory; Csikszentmihalyi's Flow adds the experiential mechanism — a task must sit in the narrow channel between boredom (too easy) and anxiety (too hard), which maps directly onto Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development. Feedback must be informational ("here's how to improve") not controlling ("here's your grade") to sustain intrinsic motivation.

Pink motivatorMechanism in this LRD
AutonomyTeams choose their own topic, data sources, and agentic-system architecture — together, not in role-split halves; the gate never dictates how to solve the checkpoint, only whether the thinking behind it is visible.
MasteryGates are binary and calibrated per week (Part 6.3) so a team is never blocked by a target it has no realistic path to — the checkpoint artifact scales with what that week's content actually taught, keeping teams inside the flow channel rather than the anxiety or boredom bands.
PurposeEvery checkpoint is real handbook/platform work, not a rehearsal exercise — the "why" is never abstract (§Motivation and Flow, structural manifestations: Challenge Based Learning's Engage→Investigate→Act arc is the same shape as a gated week).

2.3 UnBlooms — grade the thinking, not the output

Austin (2026) argues that generative AI collapsed the assumption baked into every Bloom pyramid — that you must climb through Remember/Understand/Apply before you can Create. A student can now open an AI tool and skip straight to a polished artifact. Her response is not to invert the pyramid but to replace it with a metacognitive reflection loop — Questioning → Generating → Critiquing → Refining, organised around human agency and curiosity as the thing being protected, with "AI on the sidelines" providing productive friction through Socratic tutoring. The assessment question is never what did you produce; it is how deliberately did you think along the way.

Design axiom: this is the direct ancestor of the Socratic AI agent in Part 6.4. It is why that agent is contractually barred from ever telling a student their work is good, bad, or scored — verdicts short-circuit the reflection loop UnBlooms exists to protect. The loop's four stages (Questioning / Generating / Critiquing / Refining) map onto this LRD's cycle: the agent questions, the team generates a revision, a peer team critiques, the team refines — and only then does the gate open. This matters more, not less, now that the chapter itself is machine-generated (Part 6.1): a team's own agentic system can already reach Create in Bloom's terms without the team doing any of the underlying thinking — exactly the collapse UnBlooms describes. The gate is what forces the thinking back in.

2.4 Durable skills — the capability being protected

Globerson et al. (2026), synthesised on the AI Wiki's Durable Skills page, define a durable skill by three criteria: open-ended (not reducible to closed-form rules), socially/contextually situated (its performance depends on a specific group, organisation or domain), and slow-changing (it doesn't go obsolete every 18 months). The canonical cluster is Collaboration, Creativity and Critical Thinking — and Critical Thinking now explicitly includes AI-Supported Exploration and AI-Supported Verification as sub-skills: judging AI output is itself a durable skill, not a stopgap until the model gets better. McKinsey Global Institute's Skill Change Index (2025) corroborates from the labour-market side: judgment, negotiation and mentorship sit in the lowest-automation-exposure quartile, while narrow digital skills sit in the highest.

Design axiom: this is not a fourth independent framework bolted onto Bloom, Flow and UnBlooms — it is the answer to which capability those three are protecting. Every learning outcome in Part 3 is tagged with the durable skill it practises (§3.2–3.3), and the gate itself (Part 6.3) is reframed as weekly, structural practice of two specific sub-skills: AI-Supported Verification and Collaboration.

Part 3 — Learning outcomes (Bloom-tagged)

Verbs are drawn from the revised-taxonomy verb bank (§2.1). [Process / Knowledge] tags follow Anderson & Krathwohl's two dimensions. Every outcome is phrased as a user story — a student can <do>, so <value> — the same actor + goal + value shape used elsewhere in the LRD template family (see LRD_Ontwerpende_Onderzoeksvragen_v2.docx.md §4.1). The value clause is anchored to the student's future professional role (§3.1), not to the handbook artifact alone — the artifact is the practice ground, the role is the point. A third tag, Durable skill, names which §2.4 sub-skill the outcome practises — the capability that role will still need after the model underneath it changes.

Part 4 — Target audience & prior knowledge

There is no discipline-specific admittance requirement — the minor is open to any student with at least three years of prior university study. Two populations naturally apply: business-oriented students preparing for an AI- and data-driven environment, and IT/Engineering students seeking deeper Machine Learning knowledge who also value the business modules. Module choice is not a re-run of a student's degree — the programme encourages students to take the role that stretches them. A business-background student may choose AEAIS to build real ML/coding fluency, so they can collaborate with an engineer as a peer, not a translator; an engineering-background student may choose DIAIA to build genuine business fluency.

Part 5 — Design criteria

5.1 KISS, stated as constraints

"Keep It Super Simple" is not an aspiration in this LRD — it is a set of things this system is not allowed to become, because each of the alternatives was considered and rejected during design:

  • No adaptive-difficulty algorithm. A tiered-branching or Socratic-depth-ladder system was considered and rejected in favour of a single binary gate per checkpoint — one mechanism, one rule, no scoring model to maintain or explain.
  • No student-visible scores. Students see open/locked and Socratic questions. Nothing numeric. This is a direct consequence of §2.3 (UnBlooms) as much as of KISS.
  • One team, not two paired teams. Business and technical members work inside a single team producing a single artifact — not two role-split teams reviewing each other. A separate, single peer-review partnership (one other team, stable for 7 weeks) is where structured outside feedback comes from instead (Part 6.5); rotating peer pools were considered, for exposure diversity, and rejected as unnecessary complexity.
  • One shared Socratic-agent component, instructor-owned, not built by anyone it grades. The module owners build and maintain it once (Part 6.4); every team consumes the same instance. No parallel, per-team or student-built tutoring systems, and no student control over the thing that gates them.
  • One shared rubric, not per-team custom criteria. Instructors publish one quality/design bar (Part 6.2) that every team's output is measured against — no bespoke grading standard to maintain per team.
  • Reuse existing infrastructure. The AI Wiki / Quartz template already specified in programme.html §3.2 is the platform. This LRD adds a gating layer and a peer-review-partner registry on top of it — it does not stand up new shared infrastructure. Each team's own agentic system is small, disposable, and theirs alone to run.

5.2 What "manageable" means for a teacher

A single instructor dashboard (Part 6.6) must answer, for every team, in under a minute: where are they, is the gate open, has an override been used, does the peer feedback look genuine. If a teacher cannot answer those four questions in under a minute per team, the system has failed its own KISS test — regardless of how pedagogically elegant it is.

Part 6 — The integrated learning-path system

This is the new mechanism this LRD contributes. Every team runs the same production pipeline (6.1) against the same published bar (6.2), and can only advance through a gate (6.3) built from two checks: a Socratic AI tutor (6.4) and a cross-team peer partnership (6.5). An instructor dashboard (6.6) makes it all visible.

6.1 The team's production pipeline

Each week, a team turns one input into one output: it picks (or refines) the topic and data for that week's chapter section, feeds both into its own agentic system, and the system generates the section. This is a genuine pipeline, not a writing exercise with an AI assist bolted on — the team's job is to curate the inputs and engineer the system, not to hand-write the prose. DIAIA members lead the topic and data choices and critically vet what comes out; AEAIS members build and tune the system that produces it — but both roles sit inside the one team, and the one weekly checkpoint is the generated section itself.

This is deliberately different from the shared platform infrastructure (§1.2): every team builds and owns its own pipeline; no team runs another team's system.

6.2 Shared quality & design criteria

Before week 1, instructors publish one quality/design bar — the same criteria every team's output is measured against, no bespoke rubric per team. It reuses the existing chapter rubric (programme.html §5.1: clarity, accuracy, responsible-AI coverage, usefulness to an SME owner) plus a matching design bar for the system itself (harness soundness, evaluation coverage). Two things happen with it:

  • The peer gate (§6.5) uses it directly — a peer team reviews this week's generated section against the published criteria, not against its own judgement of what's good.
  • A well-engineered team's own pipeline checks against it automatically, before submission — this is what AEAIS's evaluation-pipeline learning outcome (§3.3 #3) is for. Teams that skip this find out from their peer team instead, a week later.

Publishing the bar up front is itself a KISS move: it replaces "what does the instructor want" guessing with one fixed target, known from day one and unchanged mid-cohort (NFR-07).

6.3 Gated progression

Each of the 7 weeks has exactly one checkpoint artifact per team: that week's generated chapter section (§6.1). A team cannot start the next week's checkpoint until both conditions are met on the current one:

Gate conditionWho/what checks itWhat "met" meansDurable skill (§2.4)
A — Socratic gateThe Socratic AI agent (6.4)The team has submitted a written response to the agent's questions that is a genuine revision, not a resubmission — the hidden rubric's process dimension (not polish) clears a fixed threshold.Critical Thinking — AI-Supported Verification
B — Peer gateThe peer team (6.5)The team has both given and received one round of feedback with its peer team on this week's checkpoint, against the shared criteria (§6.2).Collaboration — Conflict Resolution, Project Management

Naming this matters: gate condition A drills the exact sub-skill — AI-Supported Verification — that the durable-skills literature (§2.4) flags as most at risk of atrophying without deliberate practice; gate condition B drills Collaboration's two most measurable sub-skills, every week, for seven weeks.

Both conditions, no partial credit, no numeric composite. The Socratic side is deliberately single-shot: one report per team per week (§6.4), not an unlimited resubmit-until-it-passes loop. If gate A doesn't clear on that one report, the team gets no second Socratic consult that week — the unresolved feedback carries into next week's submission alongside that week's new topic, and addressing it becomes part of what next week's report checks for. The peer gate (§6.5) has no such limit; a team can lean on its peer team as often as it likes between Socratic consults.

This is deliberately the "gated progression" option, not tiered branching or a depth ladder (§5.1) — the autoregression is in whether a team advances, not in which content it sees next. What varies between teams isn't how many times they can retry a single week — nobody can — it's how many weeks it takes to clear a carried-forward gate. The system rewards genuine week-over-week growth (§6.4), not speed or persistence.

A locked gate is not a penalty. It carries no grade consequence by itself (Part 9) — it is Pink's informational feedback, not controlling feedback (§2.2): a prompt to revise, not a mark against the team.

6.4 The Socratic AI agent

Owned by: the module owners/instructors — not students, not any team, not AEAIS's infrastructure build-group. The same people it evaluates cannot also control how it evaluates them; this is a deliberate exception to "AEAIS builds everything technical" (§1.2). Extends the agent already sketched in programme.html §5.3 — same dual-audience design, now also a gate condition rather than a pre-publication review step only.

  • The protocol: a team submits its checkpoint once and receives exactly one report per week — not an open-ended chat, not an unlimited resubmit-and-recheck loop. One submission, one report, once a week, per team.
  • To the team (visible): the report is Socratic questions only. Never a verdict, never "this is good/bad," never a score. Questions target the UnBlooms loop directly — why this topic and data, why this system design, and — now that the section itself is machine-generated — did the team critically vet what it produced, or just accept it.
  • To the instructor (visible): the exact same report the team received, plus a rubric score against that week's CRISP-DM phase (DIAIA) or harness/ADLC layer (AEAIS), plus the binary gate-A signal. Nothing is hidden from instructors — only from students.
  • Grows, or it isn't doing its job. Every report is generated with the team's full history of prior reports as context, under one standing instruction: never repeat a question that's already been answered, and never let a team coast on one that hasn't. A week's report that reads like a copy of last week's is a defect in the shared system, not an acceptable outcome — see Part 12 for how that's caught.

6.5 Cross-team peer network

Every team is paired, for the full 7 weeks, with exactly one other team. This is the "force optimal use of the peer network" mechanism: the peer network is not self-selected or optional — it is a structural assignment, reviewed reciprocally every week. Because both teams in a partnership already contain both roles, the review itself is naturally holistic rather than split by lens.

What the peer team checksAgainst the shared criteria (§6.2)
Business feasibilityWould an SME owner trust and use this; does it fit the "first five steps" the chapter promises?
Engineering soundnessDoes the "Recommended tooling" section reflect what the team's own system actually generated and could build, at what effort?

Because every team has both kinds of expertise on hand, no review needs a translator — the peer team checks both angles in one pass, on one artifact.

Fallback. If a team's peer-review partner falls behind or drops the module, the instructor reassigns the partnership rather than blocking the other team (FR-08, Part 7) — mirrors the persona/archetype fallback already used for SME access in programme.html §3.3.

6.6 Instructor dashboard

One screen, per team: current week, gate-A / gate-B status, this week's Socratic report and score, shared-criteria compliance from the last peer review, how many consecutive weeks (if any) gate A has carried forward, and an override control. The override exists because no binary gate should become a bureaucratic trap — an instructor can open a gate manually with one click, logged for the calibration record (Part 11, AC-07 bounds how often that should be needed).

Part 9 — Assessment alignment

Gates are formative; the criterion-based interview (CBI) stays the sole summative instrument, unchanged in shape from programme.html §5.4:

  • A locked gate never itself changes a grade. It is a revision prompt, consistent with Pink's informational-feedback principle (§2.2).
  • The Socratic agent's hidden rubric score is an instructor calibration signal only — exactly as already specified for the pre-publication review in programme.html. This LRD extends its use from "before publication" to "at every weekly gate," but does not change its evidentiary status. Instructors see the same report the team received, plus the score behind it (§6.4) — nothing about a team's weekly gate is opaque to the people grading them, only to the students being gated.
  • Two CBIs, one shared history. Each module runs its own CBI, even though the weekly work is joint. A DIAIA student defends the topic and data choices, the business case inside the generated section, and how critically they vetted what the team's system produced; an AEAIS student defends the pipeline that produced it — the harness, the architecture choice, the eval coverage. Both draw on the same exported gate/iteration history (FR-09), read through a different lens.
  • At the CBI, a student defends not just the chapter/pipeline but how they responded to the Socratic questions and to their peer team across the seven gates — directly assessing the metacognitive-knowledge outcomes in Part 3 (DIAIA #6, AEAIS #6).
  • The exported gate/iteration history (FR-09) is the evidence base for that defence — not a score sheet, a process trail.

Part 10 — Deliverables

  1. One published (curated) handbook chapter per team, generated by that team's own agentic system from the topic and data it chose — unchanged in shape from programme.html's chapter anatomy, including a "Recommended tooling" section grounded in what the team's own system actually builds.
  2. Each team's own chapter-generating agentic system (harness + knowledge architecture + eval loop + deployment/monitoring), demoed at the capstone alongside the chapter it produced.
  3. The shared platform: the hosting/dashboard built once by AEAIS's infrastructure build-group as their own capstone-within-the-capstone, and the Socratic AI tutor itself built and maintained separately by the module owners (§6.4).
  4. A per-team exported gate/iteration/feedback history (FR-09), feeding both the DIAIA and AEAIS CBI for that team's members.
  5. An instructor dashboard (Part 6.6) usable from week 1 of the pilot onward.

Part 12 — Risks & mitigations

RiskMitigation
A team defers judgment entirely to whatever its agentic system generates, rather than critically vetting itThree independent checks on the same failure mode, not one: the Socratic gate explicitly probes critical vetting of the generated output (§6.4); the shared criteria (§6.2) catch mechanical gaps the team might wave through; and the peer team (§6.5) reviews the actual generated content against those criteria, not the team's self-report of it. No single check has to catch everything.
Gate becomes a bureaucratic bottleneck / demotivatingBinary, informational framing (§6.3); one-click instructor override (§6.6); AC-07 tracks whether overrides are needed too often, which is itself a signal to loosen the threshold.
A team's peer-review partnership creates a single point of failureInstructor-triggered fallback reassignment (FR-07/08) that never blocks the unaffected team.
Teams game the Socratic agent with shallow answers to unlock fasterHidden rubric flags shallow-pattern responses to the instructor (same mechanism already specified in programme.html §5.5 for "shallow or gamed feedback"). The single-report-per-week limit (§6.4) also removes the main incentive to game it — there is no faster path through repeated resubmission to farm for.
The Socratic agent's own feedback goes stale or repetitive, undermining the growth requirement it's meant to enforceRepetition is a defect in the shared, instructor-owned system (§6.4), not a per-team problem, so it's fixed once centrally rather than 20+ times independently. AC-05 audits a sample of report histories each cohort specifically for genuine week-over-week variation, using the same spot-check discipline already applied to verdict-leakage (AC-03).
Sustained reliance on the Socratic gate erodes the verification judgment it's meant to buildStructural, not procedural: gate condition A requires a genuine revision each week, not a resubmission (§6.3) — by design incompatible with rote reliance on the agent's questions. AC-06 already requires each student to articulate, at the CBI, a specific instance where a Socratic question changed their work; a student who can't is exactly this failure mode, and it surfaces at the one summative checkpoint that matters (Part 9), without a new metric or mechanism.
AEAIS's shared-infrastructure build-group over-invests in the platform at the expense of its own six learning outcomesThe shared platform is explicitly scoped as one deliverable mapped to two of the six Sketch-3-derived outcomes (§3.3 #3, #6), sized into weeks 4–6 (contracts/eval + ADLC), not a separate project.
Quality/liability — real SMEs still read the published chaptersUnchanged from programme.html §5.5: editorial curation + "educational, not consultancy" disclaimer before anything reaches an SME. The gate system governs weekly progression, not publication.

Part 13 — Roadmap

  1. Publish the shared quality/design criteria (§6.2) before anything else — every downstream step depends on a fixed target existing first.
  2. Pilot the gate on Theme 1 only. One cohort, week 1 checkpoint only, manual instructor grading of the Socratic transcripts before trusting the hidden rubric — confirms the mechanism before it runs unattended for 7 weeks.
  3. Stand up the peer-review-partner registry and dashboard (FR-06/07) alongside the week-1 pilot.
  4. Full 7-week gated run, one cohort. Both modules, all checkpoints gated, override logging live from day one.
  5. Calibrate the hidden-rubric threshold and the shared criteria against AC-05/AC-07/AC-08 at cohort end; adjust before the next intake.
  6. Compounding. Subsequent cohorts inherit the shared platform and criteria as infrastructure — only the checkpoint content (weekly topics, harness scope) needs refreshing, per the living-artifact model already established for the handbook.

Suggested owners: course team (DIAIA + AEAIS), with AEAIS's shared-infrastructure build-group owning the hosting platform's engineering as their own deliverable each cohort, and the module owners owning the Socratic AI tutor itself (§6.4) — not delegated to any student build-group.
